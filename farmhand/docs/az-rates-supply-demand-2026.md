## The APS rate case (ACC Docket E-01345A-25-0105)

- [fact] Filed June 13, 2025: $579.5M / 13.99% net revenue increase
  requested, 10.70% ROE; raised in rebuttal to $611.3M / 14.75%. RUCO
  testimony (July 14, 2026) puts the effective residential impact ~15.3%.
- [fact] Residential class targeted for +16.44%; proposed "XHLF"
  data-center class +47.03%; large general service +23.52%. APS frames
  this as "growth pays for growth."
- [fact] APS's own estimate: ~$20/month more at 1,000 kWh (~$240/yr).
- [fact] Rate design asks: basic service charge +9.17% to +38.1% by tier;
  TOU energy charges ~+16.6%; tiered charges +14.7–16.9%; a ~$6/month
  solar Grid Access Charge (up from ~$2.29–2.56).
- [fact] June 16, 2026: the AZ Court of Appeals VACATED the 2022-era solar
  grid-access charge for lack of due process — the solar-fee piece is
  legally contested right now.
- [fact] Positions: ACC Staff $525.2M / 9.55–9.80% ROE; RUCO $200.2–278.1M
  / 9.00–9.20% ROE (opposes the Forward Rate Mechanism); AG Mayes ~3% / 6%
  ROE, calls it "blatant corporate greed" and a ~$524M/yr transfer to
  shareholders; AriSEIA/Vote Solar want the on-peak window cut to 5–8pm.
- [fact] Evidentiary hearing ran May 18 – July 7, 2026 — 31 days, the
  longest in ACC history. ALJ recommended order expected late Nov 2026;
  ACC vote required by Dec 31, 2026; new rates effective early 2027.
- [fact] Precedent: in the 2022 case APS asked 13.6% and got ~8.3%
  residential (a ~40% haircut).
- [projection] Applying the same haircut pattern → residential landing
  zone roughly 8–11%. No party forecasts this; it is pattern-matching.

## Supply and demand: growth, data centers, generation

- [fact] Census 2025 vintage: Buckeye 125,445 · Goodyear 125,359 ·
  Surprise 175,304 · Maricopa County 4,689,558 (#3 nationally in numeric
  growth 2023–24). Buckeye/Goodyear/Surprise all in the Census top-100
  fastest-growing cities for 2024.
- [fact] Teravalis (Buckeye): ~37,000 acres, planned 100,000 homes /
  300,000 residents — but only 45 homes completed as of Nov 2025. Most of
  that load is future, not present.
- [projection] MAG projects the West Valley to pass 2M residents by 2030.
- [fact] APS added 32,000+ residential meters in 2024; 2.4% customer
  growth in 2025.
- [fact] APS reports 4.5 GW of committed load beyond its 8.6 GW system
  peak, plus ~20 GW of "uncommitted opportunity" (~30 data-center
  projects) in queue; ~19 GW of data-center service requests cited to a
  House committee (Jan 2026).
- [fact] APS proposes up to 2 GW of new gas generation (Desert Sun / Gila
  Bend), Phase 1 ~late 2030; Phase 2 structured to be financed by large
  users via long-term contracts. Capital plan ~$10.35B (2025–28); rate
  base growing 6–8%/yr; $6B+ transmission through 2035.
- [projection] APS planning shows peak load growing ~40% by 2031 (8.7 GW
  toward ~12 GW by 2035). SRP forecasts ~2,300 MW (~30%) peak growth by
  2030; SRP's Coolidge expansion was scaled to 575 MW (~$775M).
- [fact] Lazard June 2025 LCOE: utility solar $38–78/MWh · combined-cycle
  gas $48–109 · solar+storage $50–131 · gas PEAKERS $149–251/MWh — the
  generation type built for data-center reliability is the most expensive.
- [contested] Do data centers raise residential bills? Two credible views:
  Harvard ELP documents cost-shifting risk nationally and a former AZ
  Energy Office director calls APS's projections "highly speculative";
  a June 2026 arXiv paper finds data-center buildout historically
  correlated with residential price DECREASES (~3.5% per doubling of
  capacity). An ASU energy historian: "You simply can't draw that causal
  line." Content must present this as genuinely unresolved.
- [interested-party] A solar-company blog claims data centers = 94% of APS
  demand growth 2023–25. Directionally consistent with APS's queue, but
  from a commercially interested source — never state as fact.

## Price history and forecasts

- [fact] AZ average residential price 2015→2024: 12.13¢ → 14.91¢/kWh
  (+22.9%). National: 12.65¢ → 16.48¢ (+30.3%). Arizona rose SLOWER than
  the national average and the discount widened (0.5¢ → 1.6¢).
- [fact] But AZ average monthly BILL is higher: $160.24 vs $142.26
  national (2024), because AZ homes use ~25% more electricity (1,074.9 vs
  863.3 kWh/mo — AC load). "Cheap by the kWh, expensive by the month."
- [fact] Utility split 2020→2024: APS 13.24¢ → 16.45¢ (+24.2%); SRP
  11.45¢ → 13.46¢ (+17.6%).
- [fact] BLS CPI electricity: +4.3% (2021), +13.1% (2022), +5.8% (2023),
  +4.2% (2024), +4.6% (2025), +4.0% y/y as of June 2026 — about double
  core inflation.
- [fact/published-projection] EIA STEO (July 2026): national residential
  16.5¢ (2024) → 18.3¢ (2026, +5.7%) → 18.7¢ (2027). EIA AEO2026 base
  case: ~18.1¢ (2030), 20.8¢ (2035) nominal — roughly FLAT in real terms.
- [projection — analysts disagree] ICF: +15–40% national by 2030.
  NC State/Carnegie Mellon: +6–29% by 2030 (some regions +57%). UCS:
  $411–524B cumulative wholesale increase 2026–35 from data centers.
  Brattle + the arXiv study find minimal-to-negative effects. Present as
  a live disagreement, never pick one as "the" forecast.
- [fact] Other AZ movement: TEP pending case ~13% (AG argues ~4%); SRP
  raised its fixed monthly service charge ~50% in Nov 2025 (to ~$30–32).

## Homeowner translation (1,800 kWh summer month)

- [estimate] Baseline bill at blended 15.5–17¢: roughly $280–305/month.
- [fact→estimate] APS's full request ≈ +$36–50/mo · Staff position ≈
  +$31–43 · 2022-style haircut (~8%) ≈ +$18–25 (most defensible central
  case, labeled projection) · RUCO ≈ +$12–22 · AG position ≈ +$8–9.
- [estimate] Each 0.1¢/kWh fuel-adjustor move ≈ ±$1.80/month at 1,800 kWh,
  independent of the rate case.
- [speculative] FRM, if approved (contested): +2–4%/yr from 2028 ≈
  +$6–12/month per year compounding.
- [contested] Data-center net effect by 2030: plausible −$3 to +$15/month.
- [projection] If AZ tracked ICF's national +15–40% by 2030, a $300 summer
  bill becomes ~$345–420 — but honest framing notes AZ has historically
  LAGGED national price growth.
- [estimate] SRP: each 1% base change ≈ ±$2.80–3.00/month on a $280–300 bill.

## The consumer-pain record (named, sourced — use respectfully)

- [fact] Named bills: Yuma senior Ruthey Phillips — $900 in one month on
  ~$4,000/mo income (AZ Capitol Times op-ed, Feb 2026). North Phoenix
  homeowner — $714 July bill, up from a $550 record (ABC15). Gilbert's
  David Gingras — ~$800 SRP projection vs typical $500 (ABC15). Allison
  Hawley, 55+ community — $359 September bill, "choosing whether they're
  going to eat that month or pay that bill" (Cronkite News, Apr 2026).
- [fact] Average AZ summer household bill projected $784 in 2025, +6.2%
  y/y, highest in a decade (Guardian/Watt Wise).
- [fact] Hearing-room record: protesters at the May 18, 2026 kickoff;
  ~4 hours of public testimony; retired educator Anna Cicero: "I cannot
  switch to a cheaper electrical company."
- [fact] The October cliff: AZ's summer disconnection moratorium (Jun 1 –
  Oct 15 for ACC-regulated utilities) pauses shutoffs but does NOT forgive
  the balance — full amount due when it lifts. Tempe CAA CEO: people
  arriving "into October with a $1,600 power bill." APS disconnected
  2,200+ customers in May 2024 alone.
- [fact] April 2026: APS settled $7M+ with the AG after a heat-related
  death linked to disconnection; agreed to halt shutoffs whenever temps
  hit 95°F. Katherine Korman, 82, died in 2024 days after disconnection;
  Stephanie Pullman died in 2018 after a shutoff over $51.
- [fact] Maricopa heat deaths: 645 (2023), 608 (2024), 430 (2025); in
  ~70% of indoor deaths AC was present but broken.
- [fact] LIHEAP inequity: AZ received $61M (2023) vs Michigan's $287M;
  <5% of eligible AZ households served vs ~90% in MI.
- [fact] Pinnacle West 2024 profit: $608.8M (up from $501.6M). Verified
  history: APS admitted spending $12.9M on 2014-cycle political groups,
  $10.7M tied to ACC races. NUANCE REQUIRED: APS is now legally barred
  from funding commissioner campaigns — cite as history, not ongoing.
- [content rule] Solar does not fix disconnection debt or heat-safety
  crises — never imply it would have prevented a named tragedy. The one
  attributable solar outcome: the Chamblee family's own account of bills
  falling from ~$400 to ~$60 after installing solar (Guardian, Aug 2025)
  — citable as THEIR outcome, never as a general promise.
